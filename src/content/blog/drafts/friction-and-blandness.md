I went back to playing World of Warcraft (WoW) recently. It is a game that I've played for years almost non stop, basically since it launched until the release of the [Legion expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_of_Warcraft:_Legion) around 2016.

It is a game that was part of my teenage years and I have found memories of. But coming back it is a completely different game, not unexpectedly, but it is kinda sad. It feels weird playing the game now, not because it is hard, complex, or full of anachronistic game decisions. I was expecting all that.
After all WoW is more than 20 years old, it has some design decisions that made sense at that point in time but don't make sense anymore. The people playing the game have changed, so the expectations on how the game should behave have changed too.

The two main reasons that made me return were:
1. I was really curious to find out how much time I had played, and I needed to log in to find that out.
2. I just wanted to live the story that I have missed.

## On quality of life improvements and simplification

I was presented to the interface I kinda knew, full of buttons and stuff. Cool. I'm kinda at home.

Some things have changed, the interface looks more sleek and "modern". And that was a bummer for me, as the [skeumorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skeuomorph) interface was something I really liked. But now as I'm a developer I do understand the tradeoffs of a detailed interface and scaling it, the needs of assets and all. So, a bummer but not a huge deal.

One of WoW's biggest strengths in my opinion is that it allows you to have AddOns. Those are pieces of code that you run on the game, they can't do things that would break the game, but they are super powerful and make life easier[^1].

So, some of the interface changes meant I didn't need the trusty old addons I used to use. Things were baked into the game now. I ended up with a large list of addons anyways but I digress. The point is: doing some things in the game was easier, and it was for the better.

But doing some other things on the game were not necessarily easier, they were just "simpler"... That showed up when I had to select my character's talents, I knew it would influence the skills and things I would have access to, but it felt off, streamlined, yes, but also [_railroading_](https://wiki.rpg.net/index.php/Railroading).

Like, yes, I had 3 main options of how my character would specialize, and that gave me some agency, but in those options I couldn't make mistakes, I couldn't make a "sub-optimal" build, I couldn't select "the wrong things".

At the same time, things were confusing given the structure of the talent tree less like a "tree" with branches and more like an intertwined mess. Arrows go everywhere and connect to every other node. It feels both freeing and constraining.

Then I started exploring the world...

## Onboarding and returning

Onboarding someone is hard, like, it is a complex situation. Welcoming back someone is even harder, specially on a world full of lore and in an ever evolving state.

Things change, so how to deal with someone who is returning?

I was met with a notification to do a "catch up" quest chain. From the text it said I would be able to level a little bit and get better gear to handle the latest expansion. Okay, sounds good.

The quest felt confusing, not due to the back and forth of "go fetch 5 McGuffins somewhere far from here" or "talk to these two NPCs back and forth like I'm a postal officer". That I was expecting.

The quests were just bland, the writing of the story felt unimportant, disconnected, I was doing that just for the sake of doing the thing. If that's the case, why not give me the gear already and skip the quest?
There were no mechanics changes for me to catch up, there was only a couple of fights to adapt to my skill rotation (a.k.a.: the sequence of skills to use).
And that was a frustrating grind because I was underpowered due to the lack of gear.

Again, returning to a game is hard. It could be worse, [Fallout 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fallout_3), [New Vegas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fallout:_New_Vegas), [Fallout 4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fallout_4), and [Fallout 76](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fallout_76) spam you with quest notifications when you add their expansions/DLCs. So you don't know what to do first or where to get started.

[Warframe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warframe) was an even worse, as I had a completely broken experience: too underpowered to do current content, stuck with old things, and with half completed, half available options of what to do.

But still, why can't I pick up the game where I goddamn left it?! Aren't the expansions supposed to be _sequential_?!

Now I know the quest chain I did was connected to some part of the latest expansion but as I haven't experience the previous couple expansions, those are completely meaningless to me. Yes, I know how [Jaina](https://wowpedia.fandom.com/wiki/Jaina_Proudmoore) and [Thrall](https://wowpedia.fandom.com/wiki/Thrall) are, I've known them since Warcraft 3... But their current struggle is completely new and irrelevant to me, I don't have the emotional connection to it.

Then Youtube picked up I was back to playing, because of course it did and [some](https://youtu.be/1TZ-_dlxiBY?si=PtY6YmD_14NWfYNy) [videos](https://youtu.be/BCjFkz3FP3M?si=bcSk3MBhV_2Hij8G) showed up... It is not only me, I guess.

## Careful what you wish for...

Then this video showed up: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EawFAMLsnXg

One of the things it mentioned and what made everything click to me is the comment from Blizzard developers that "people don't want vanilla WoW".

It is interesting that the argument for the state of the game nowadays is that "players _asked_ for those changes". So to me it sounds like a [Monkey's Paw](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Monkey%27s_Paw): we need to be careful what we wish for... Or have we now?

## Usability and removing friction

When we read or study usability, the core principle is to make the user's life easier, make it effortless for them to complete their tasks. That usually means removing _friction_ on the design of things. The problem is people usually forget other things like hierarchy of information or disambiguation[^2] and end up defaulting to just removing friction. It is easy, it is the low hanging fruit, it makes investors happy.

Problem is: video games _are_ about friction.
The core loop of a video game is:
1. Introducing a challenge that the player has to overcome
2. Providing the means to overcome that challenge.
3. Mix up with other challenges/means to overcome them.
4. Rinse and repeat.

When we remove friction the game becomes boring, it becomes bland, the dopamine hit of the reward becomes meaningless because we didn't made an effort to get the thing, the prize was handed out to us.

Then it clicked again...

## Enshitification

The wave of enshitification that we are seeing, or why things are so bland and boring right now are a result of that in my opinion. The platforms we use are over optimized to remove friction and give us gratification for free
